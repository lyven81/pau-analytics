# Do Dollars Drive Decisions? What Loan Data Reveals About Pricing and Bias

A retail shop owner in Petaling Jaya gives his loyal customers 30-day credit terms. New customers pay upfront. He would tell you it's based on trust built over time — and he would probably be right. But if you asked him to prove it with data, could he?

That is the question Michael, who managed the loan department at a regional bank, had to answer when lower-income applicants complained they were receiving worse loan terms than wealthier borrowers with similar credit profiles. He had a gut feeling the gap was justified. The data told a more complicated story.

In this post, we look at what four years of loan data revealed about income-based pricing — where the gap was defensible, where unconscious bias was quietly distorting decisions, and what any business owner can learn from it.

## The Income Gap That Started the Investigation

The data was clear from the first query. High-income applicants received an average interest rate of 10.90%. Low-income applicants received 11.99% — a gap of 1.09 percentage points. Over the life of a RM 200,000 loan, that difference runs into tens of thousands of ringgit.

The first question was obvious: was this fair?

The second question was harder: how would you even know?

Michael had a problem that every business owner who makes customer-tier decisions faces. The outcome — different prices for different customers — looks like bias from the outside. But it might be completely justified by the data. The only way to know is to check.

## When Wealth Does Not Predict the Outcome

One finding stopped Michael's team early. Income and loan size had only a weak correlation of 0.37. High-income applicants did not automatically receive bigger loans. Credit scores, existing debt, collateral, and payment history mattered more than salary.

This challenged a common assumption: that wealthier customers are automatically better customers.

Think about how this plays out in a retail business. If you offer credit terms to customers who look successful — based on how they present themselves, what car they drive, what company they claim to represent — you are making a decision based on perceived wealth, not proven reliability. Those two things are not the same. The data showed that income alone was a poor predictor of actual financial behaviour.

## The Hidden Bias in Credit Grades

The loan files included credit grades from A (lowest risk) to E (highest risk). High-income applicants clustered in A and B grades. Low-income applicants clustered in C, D, and E.

This looked like a justified risk segmentation — until Michael examined how those grades were being assigned.

Loan officers could see applicant income during the grading process. Officers consistently gave higher grades to wealthier applicants, even when the underlying financial ratios were similar. A large salary triggered unconscious positive bias. A modest salary triggered unconscious skepticism — even when the debt ratios and payment histories were solid.

Two applicants with identical debt-to-income ratios were receiving different grades. The only difference was income. That is not risk assessment. That is assumption dressed up as risk assessment.

## What Actually Justified the Gap

Not everything in the data pointed to bias. This is important: the interest rate gap was real — and it was largely defensible.

High-income borrowers genuinely had lower debt-to-income ratios. They used less of their available credit. These are hard, objective measures of financial risk. Lower risk borrowers do deserve lower rates. That is not bias — it is data.

There is a distinction that matters for any business owner who sets tiered prices or credit terms:

- **Justified differentiation:** Different prices because the data shows different risk levels or different value delivered
- **Unjustified assumption:** Different prices because the customer looks a certain way

Both can produce the same outcome. Only data can tell you which one is driving your decisions.

The interest rate gap survived scrutiny. The credit grade distribution did not.

## How Removing Income From the Process Changed Things

Michael made one structural change. During the initial grading phase, loan officers could no longer see applicant income. They assessed debt-to-income ratio, credit score, payment history, and employment stability — not salary figures.

He also replaced subjective grading with standardised thresholds. If two applicants had the same debt-to-income ratio and credit score, they received the same grade. Human judgment remained for edge cases, but the starting point became objective.

Six months later, the interest rate gap persisted at 10.9% versus 12.0%, because the underlying risk difference was real. But the credit grade distribution shifted. More low-income applicants with strong financials received A and B grades. Fewer high-income applicants with mediocre debt ratios received automatic As. The grades now reflected risk — not income levels.

The fix was not to eliminate income as a factor. It was to put it in the right place in the process.

## What This Means for Your Business

You may not run a loan department. But if you offer different prices, payment terms, discounts, or service levels to different customers, the same principle applies.

Ask these questions:

- Do you extend credit terms to certain customers? Is that based on their actual payment history — or on how long you have known them?
- Do some customers receive better pricing? Is there a clear, data-backed rule for who qualifies — or does it depend on who asks?
- Do your most trusted customers continue to receive preferential treatment even after their order patterns have changed?

Pricing decisions feel intuitive. Intuition is built from experience — and experience can carry biases that data will surface.

Michael's bank lends on data now, not demographics. Complaints dropped. Regulatory reviews found no bias. The lending process became transparent and defensible.

The question for your business is simpler. If you had to explain every pricing decision with data, could you? If not, the gap between what you think is driving your decisions and what is actually driving them may be worth finding out.
