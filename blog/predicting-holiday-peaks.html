How to Time Your Christmas Campaign: What 8 Years of Streaming Data Reveals

Published 25 May 2026

25 May 2026 EN

Most businesses plan their Christmas campaigns by the calendar. The holiday season is eight weeks long — mid-November to early January — so that is how long they run their marketing. Budget spread evenly. Content posted consistently. Teams stretched from November to the new year.

The data says the real window is three weeks.

If you run seasonal campaigns for your business, knowing when your audience is actually engaged could be the single most useful thing you learn this year. This post breaks down what eight years of holiday engagement data revealed — and how one marketing manager used it to restructure a RM300,000 campaign.

Why Timing Matters More Than You Think

Ahmad managed marketing for a retail mall in Kuala Lumpur. Every November, he launched his Christmas campaign: festive music, decorations, events, and promotions. He spread his RM300,000 budget across eight weeks from mid-November to early January.

He was not guessing at quality. The content was good. The promotions were competitive. But he had no idea when his audience was actually paying attention.

His assumption was the same one most seasonal businesses make: the holiday season is the holiday season. People are in a festive mood from November onwards. That is the window you need to fill.

The data showed something different.

What 8 Years of Streaming Revealed

A data analyst studied eight years of Christmas music streaming from 2017 to 2025. Weekly stream counts were tracked for popular holiday tracks like "All I Want for Christmas Is You" and "Last Christmas" across every week of every year. The goal was simple: find out when people actually started engaging with holiday content.

The pattern that came out of the data was striking.

From January through October, streams sat near zero. There was no holiday mood building slowly across the year. In mid-November, a gradual rise began — interest was growing, but modestly.

Weeks 50 to 52 — the final three weeks of December leading into Christmas — that is where engagement peaked. Stream counts jumped sharply. Mariah Carey's "All I Want for Christmas Is You" generated its highest numbers in these exact weeks, year after year. Classic tracks like "Jingle Bell Rock" and "Feliz Navidad" followed the same pattern.

Then the moment January started, streams dropped back to near zero within days.

This pattern repeated every single year from 2017 to 2025. Eight years. No exceptions. The analyst built a time-series model to confirm the finding was not random noise. The result was the same every time: mid-November marks the start, weeks 50 to 52 deliver the peak, and early January is zero. The seasonal pattern was 100% predictable.

Why This Matters for Campaign Budget Decisions

If you are spending marketing budget across an eight-week holiday season, you are likely over-investing in weeks when your audience is not yet paying attention — and under-investing in the three-week window when they are fully engaged.

Ahmad's previous budget allocation looked like this: roughly even spending across eight weeks, about RM37,500 going into each one. Some of that budget was landing in early November when holiday engagement was still flat. Some was running into January after the festive mood had already collapsed.

A clothing retailer in Subang Jaya discovered the same timing issue when she looked at her own end-of-year data. Her Christmas promotions ran for seven and a half weeks. When she pulled her actual transaction records, she found that 65% of her Christmas-period revenue came in during the final three weeks of December. She had been treating a three-week peak as a seven-week season.

The cost was not just money wasted on quiet weeks. It was staff overtime, promotion fatigue, and email lists burned out before the real peak arrived.

How Ahmad Restructured His Campaign

After seeing the streaming analysis, Ahmad rebuilt his entire campaign calendar from scratch. Before the data, his budget had been spread evenly. After the data, it followed real audience behaviour:

  • Mid-November (warm-up): 20% of budget — light brand awareness, early festive content
  • Late November build-up: 10% of budget — increasing frequency, teaser promotions
  • Weeks 50–52 (peak window): 60% of budget — full campaign intensity, heavy promotions, event activations
  • Early January: 0% — campaign stops immediately when engagement drops

The peak window went from receiving 30% of the budget to receiving 60%. Not because Ahmad spent more overall, but because he stopped spreading budget across weeks when nobody was paying attention.

He also changed how he tracked Christmas music trends year on year. Because the streaming data was updated weekly, he could see which songs were trending upward in the current year and adjust his mall's playlist and promotional content accordingly. The blueprint updated itself every Christmas.

What This Means for Your Business

The streaming data is specific to Christmas campaigns. But the principle it demonstrates applies to any business with a predictable seasonal peak — Hari Raya, Chinese New Year, back-to-school, school holidays. You are almost certainly working with a shorter, sharper window than your campaign calendar assumes.

Three practical takeaways from Ahmad's experience:

Find your actual peak window, not your assumed one. Pull two or three years of sales or booking data for your busiest season. Identify which specific weeks account for the majority of revenue. It is almost always shorter than businesses assume.

Reallocate your budget to follow the data. Once you know your real window, shift spending towards it. This does not mean spending more overall — it means concentrating what you already have in the weeks that count.

Track leading signals, not just lagging results. Ahmad used streaming trends to tell him when his audience was warming up before mall footfall data could. In your business, there may be early signals — website searches, enquiry volume, social engagement — that show you where attention is heading before the cash register tells you.

Ahmad no longer guesses at Christmas campaign timing. He launches in mid-November with modest awareness spending, ramps up hard in the final three December weeks, and stops the moment January arrives. Eight years of data gave him a blueprint. His budget now follows real audience behaviour, not a calendar assumption.
