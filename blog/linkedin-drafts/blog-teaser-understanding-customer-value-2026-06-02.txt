Fewer than 2% of customers were generating almost all the profit.

The bicycle shop had 18,400 customers and more than 60,000 transactions. Business looked healthy. But profit was not growing to match the activity.

When we analysed the full customer base by profit contribution, the imbalance was stark:

- High-value customers: $10.6 million in total profit
- Medium-value customers: $1.4 million
- Low-value customers: $0.12 million

The high-value tier — people who bought complete bicycles and premium upgrades — generated 87 times more profit than the frequent accessory buyers at the bottom. And nobody in the business had known who they were.

The shop had been running promotions, campaigns, and loyalty rewards aimed at volume. None of it was targeted at the customers who actually mattered.

Most businesses track how often customers visit. Very few track how much profit each customer contributes over time. Those are very different numbers — and they point to very different decisions.
