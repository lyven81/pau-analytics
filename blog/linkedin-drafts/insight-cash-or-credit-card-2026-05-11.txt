Statistically significant does not always mean practically important.

A fashion retailer spent months debating whether to push credit card payments. Her store managers believed credit card customers spent more. The data confirmed it — but the story it told was not the one they expected.

We analysed 3,400 transactions across five stores over a year. Credit card customers did spend more on average: $77.62 versus $72.21 for cash. The difference was statistically significant. The p-value was 0.035.

But the real difference was $7.67.

That is a Cohen's d of 0.018 — a tiny effect. Statistically real, practically negligible.

It got more interesting when we looked at monthly patterns. In November, cash customers outspent credit card customers by $42. In December, credit card customers outspent cash customers by $76. The pattern flipped every month because seasonal promotions were doing the heavy lifting — not payment method.

Payment method ranked near the bottom as a predictor of customer value. Total spend, purchase frequency, and product category mattered far more.

The retailer stopped training staff to push credit cards. She shifted to tracking purchase behavior instead. High-value customers were now defined by how often they came back and how much they spent — not how they paid.

Before you build a strategy around a "significant" finding, check whether the effect is actually large enough to change anything.
