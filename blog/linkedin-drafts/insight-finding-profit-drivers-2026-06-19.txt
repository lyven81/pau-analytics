Your highest-margin product may not be your most profitable one.

That sounds wrong. Most retail owners track gross margin percentage as the key number — if something sells at 60% margin, it must be contributing well. But margin percentage and absolute profit are two different things, and confusing them is one of the most common profit mistakes in retail.

We analysed over 60,000 transactions for a bicycle retailer. Accessories — helmets, gloves, cycling gear — were carrying margins above 60%. On paper, they looked like the stars of the range.

But because their unit prices were low, the actual ringgit profit per sale was small. A RM30 item at 60% margin delivers RM18. A single premium mountain bike at 20% margin on a RM4,000 sale delivers RM800. The accessories looked healthy. They were quietly diluting overall profit.

A small group of high-value bike models was responsible for the bulk of the shop's profitability. The manager had been spreading budget, shelf space, and promotion effort across the full range without knowing this.

The fix was not to stop selling accessories. It was to stop treating them as if they drove profit — and to focus pricing discipline and promotion effort on the products that actually moved the needle.

Check your own numbers. Rank your products not by margin percentage, but by the actual profit each one delivers per sale. The ranking might surprise you.
