How 30 Customer Reviews Can Predict Which Shopee Products Will Take Off

4 May 2026 EN

Most Shopee sellers decide which products to promote based on past sales. If something sold well last month, it gets the ad budget this month. That logic feels right. But there is a more reliable signal hiding in your product data — one that tells you which products are about to grow, not just which ones already have.

When we analysed sales and rating data for a Malaysian Shopee retailer across their full product catalogue, the clearest predictor of future sales was not price, product category, or marketing spend. It was customer ratings. This post breaks down what the data showed, where the tipping points are, and how to use this to focus your promotional effort.

Ratings and Sales Move Together — Almost Perfectly

The first finding was striking. There was an almost one-to-one relationship between the number of ratings a product had received and the number of units it had sold. Not a rough correlation — a steady, predictable match across the entire catalogue.

This makes intuitive sense: ratings build social proof, social proof drives conversions, and conversions generate more ratings. But for most sellers, this feedback loop runs in the background unmanaged. The data showed it can be managed deliberately.

Three Thresholds Where Sales Accelerate

The relationship between ratings and sales is not a straight line. It has clear acceleration points.

Products with fewer than 1,000 ratings grew slowly. They were selling, but not compounding.

Products that crossed 1,000 ratings averaged around 2,400 units sold. That is where meaningful growth begins.

Products that reached 10,000 ratings averaged around 23,000 units sold. At this stage, the feedback loop is self-sustaining: reviews bring buyers, buyers bring reviews.

A clothing retailer in Petaling Jaya had 15 products sitting below 300 ratings for nearly a year. She had been dividing her ad spend equally across all of them. Once she saw the rating thresholds, she redirected 70% of her budget to the five products closest to 1,000 ratings. Within two months, three crossed the threshold — and their monthly sales nearly doubled.

The 30-Review Window That Changes Everything

The most practically useful finding was how early the turning point arrives.

With just 30 to 50 ratings, a product's probability of becoming a high seller jumps to almost 100 percent. The critical window is the 20 to 30 rating mark — where the odds shift decisively.

This means the earliest stage of a product's life on Shopee is disproportionately important. Getting a new product through the first 30 reviews is more valuable than any amount of advertising on a product that has not cleared that threshold.

The retailer tested this directly. They offered small post-purchase incentives for reviews on new products and focused early promotions on reaching 30 ratings, not on generating immediate sales. Products that reached 30 reviews faster moved to the growth stage faster.

How to Restructure Your Promotional Budget Around Ratings

Once you see ratings as a leading indicator, the budget decision becomes clearer.

Products with 0 to 30 ratings: The priority is reviews, not reach. Small discounts to early buyers, simple post-purchase follow-up messages, patience. Do not pour ad spend here — ratings are the bottleneck.

Products with 30 to 1,000 ratings: This is where promotion pays off most directly. These products have social proof and need visibility to compound. Standard ads and Shopee boosts work well at this stage.

Products approaching 1,000 ratings: The jump in average sales at this threshold is large enough to justify a targeted push. A short burst of heavier promotion to cross 1,000 ratings can have lasting effects on a product's trajectory.

What This Means for Your Business

Customer ratings are not a marketing afterthought on Shopee. They are the clearest leading indicator in your product data — more predictive of future sales than last month's revenue.

Three things to take from this:

  1. Know where each of your products stands in the rating stages. A product with 800 ratings and 500 sales has a very different trajectory than a product with 50 ratings and 500 sales.
  2. Focus early-stage effort on the review bottleneck, not the sales bottleneck. The sales follow once the reviews are there.
  3. Watch for products approaching 1,000 ratings. That crossing is worth targeting directly.
