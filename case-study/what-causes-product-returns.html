How a Warehouse Manager Uncovered Why Products Get Returned

The warehouse manager at a Malaysian e-commerce home appliance retailer was watching returns pile up. Returned items were eating into margins and slowing down warehouse operations, but no one could say with confidence why customers were sending products back.

Before changing any policy, the manager wanted a clear list of suspects. Pau Analytics took the question on and pulled together the shop's product, pricing, rating, and stock data to see which factors were actually linked to returns — and which were not.

What the Data Showed

Two factors stood out as weak but real signals. Products sold with bigger discounts came back slightly more often, and higher-priced items also had a slightly higher return rate. Both links were small, not strong, but they pointed to the same area: pricing and promotion decisions.

Three other suspects were cleared. Customer ratings and the number of reviews showed no meaningful relationship with returns. Stock availability was also ruled out — items that had been out of stock were returned at the same rate as items that stayed in stock the whole time. Quality perception and warehouse stockouts were not the cause.

The most important finding was about the data itself. A simple prediction tool built from rating, discount, and review count alone could not reliably flag high-return products. It spotted the easy cases but missed the risky ones — telling the team that the real reasons sit in data they had not yet collected: actual return reasons, delivery issues, past return history, and customer complaints.

What Changed

Instead of jumping to a fix, the manager treated the report as a diagnosis. The suspect list was narrowed to pricing and promotion, quality and stock were taken off the table, and the team agreed not to make policy changes until they could prove the cause.

The bigger shift was operational. The warehouse began logging return reasons at intake — "changed my mind", "wrong size", "damaged on arrival", "not as described" — and started joining delivery records and customer complaint tickets back to the product data. For the first time, every returned item now leaves a trail explaining why it came back.

The Result

The manager now has a clear map of where returns come from and where they don't. Discount and pricing decisions are being watched closely on high-ticket appliances, and the new return-reason log is steadily growing into the dataset needed to confirm the real causes — and eventually flag risky products before they ever leave the warehouse.
