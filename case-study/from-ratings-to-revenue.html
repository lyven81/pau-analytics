Turning Reviews Into Sales

An e-commerce retailer in Malaysia selling on Shopee wanted to understand why some of their products took off while others stalled on the shelf. The owner suspected customer reviews played a role, but had no clear picture of how much they really mattered.

To get a real answer, the retailer brought the question to Pau Analytics. Together they looked at sales and rating data across the shop's product catalogue to see what the numbers actually said.

What the Data Showed

The first finding was striking: there was an almost one-to-one match between the number of ratings a product received and the units it sold. More ratings meant more sales, in a steady and predictable way.

The second finding showed how growth speeds up as ratings build. Products with fewer than 1,000 ratings grew slowly. Once a product crossed 1,000 ratings, average sales jumped to around 2,400 units. Products that reached 10,000 ratings averaged about 23,000 units sold.

The most surprising insight was how early the turning point happened. With just 30 to 50 customer ratings, a product's chance of becoming a high seller jumped to almost 100 percent. The 20 to 30 rating mark was the critical window where the odds began to swing.

What Changed

Armed with these numbers, the retailer reshaped how they launched and promoted products. Instead of spreading effort thinly, they focused on getting every new product to the 30-rating mark as quickly as possible through small discounts and post-purchase follow-ups.

Products sitting between 10 and 30 ratings were given the heaviest promotional push, while products approaching the 1,000-rating threshold received extra ad spend to carry them across the line. Rating targets were built into every new product launch from day one.

The Result

The retailer now treats ratings as a leading indicator of sales, not a vanity metric. Promotion budgets are aimed at the rating thresholds where the data shows the biggest jumps, and new products move through the early review stage faster than before — turning customer feedback into a steady engine for sales growth.
